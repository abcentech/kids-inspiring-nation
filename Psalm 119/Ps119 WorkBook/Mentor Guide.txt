## WELCOME, MENTOR!

### Thank You for Supporting a National Builder!

You are playing a **critical role** in developing the next generation of Nigerian leaders. By mentoring a young person (or team) through this project, you're not just helping them complete a challenge - you're shaping their character, building their confidence, and teaching them that they can make a difference.

**This guide will help you:**
- Understand your role and boundaries
- Support youth effectively without doing the work for them
- Navigate challenges that arise
- Foster values development
- Keep participants safe
- Know when to step in and when to step back

### Who Are NBC Mentors?

Mentors come from all backgrounds:
- Teachers supervising student teams
- Parents supporting their children
- Youth pastors/imams guiding church/mosque youth
- Community leaders sponsoring local projects
- Professionals volunteering their expertise
- Alumni of past NBC challenges

**What matters most:** Your commitment to guiding (not directing) young people as they discover their capacity to solve problems.

### What Makes a Good Mentor?

✅ **PATIENT** - Youth will make mistakes; that's how they learn  
✅ **ENCOURAGING** - They need your belief in them  
✅ **AVAILABLE** - Regular check-ins, responsive to questions  
✅ **HUMBLE** - You don't have all the answers  
✅ **WISE** - You help them think, not think for them  
✅ **COMMITTED** - You'll stick with them for 10 months  

**You don't need to be an expert.** You just need to care.

## WHAT IS NATIONAL BUILDERS CORP?

### The Big Picture

National Builders Corp (NBC) is a 10-month challenge (March-December) where Nigerian youth ages 7-17 solve REAL community problems using universal values and resourcefulness.

**Mission:** Raise a generation of young Nigerians who embody values and solve problems without waiting for government or external help.

**2025 Goals:**
- 1,000 youth participants
- 200-300 projects implemented
- 7-10 finalists selected for Grand Finale
- ₦3,000,000 in prizes awarded

### How It Works

**March:** Registration (simple 10-minute form)

**April:** Values Training (youth study 8 core values)

**May-October:** Project Implementation (6 months of action)
- Youth identify community problems
- Design and implement solutions
- Document their work
- Submit monthly progress reports

**November:** Judging (experts review all projects)

**December 17:** Grand Finale in Abuja
- 7-10 finalists present projects
- ₦3M in prizes awarded:
  - Grand Champion: ₦1,000,000
  - 1st Runner-up: ₦600,000
  - 2nd Runner-up: ₦400,000
  - Special Awards: ₦200,000 each

### The 8 Core Values

All projects are guided by these universal values:

1. **Integrity & Truth-Seeking** - "I go do am well, no shortcut"
2. **Discipline & Diligence** - "I no dey give up"
3. **Wisdom & Discernment** - "I sabi think am well"
4. **Service & Compassion** - "My brother's problem na my problem"
5. **Justice & Righteousness** - "E must fair for everybody"
6. **Perseverance & Resilience** - "Wahala no fit stop me"
7. **Humility & Learning** - "I fit learn from anybody"
8. **Excellence & Craftsmanship** - "If I wan do am, make I do am correct"

**As a mentor, your job is to help youth LIVE these values, not just recite them.**

## YOUR ROLE AS A MENTOR

### What Mentors ARE:

✅ **AN ENCOURAGER** - When they want to quit, you remind them why they started

✅ **AN ADVISOR** - When they're stuck, you ask questions that help them think

✅ **A CONNECTOR** - You link them to resources, people, and opportunities

✅ **A SAFETY MONITOR** - You keep them safe from harm

✅ **AN ACCOUNTABILITY PARTNER** - You make sure they follow through on commitments

✅ **A CHEERLEADER** - You celebrate their wins, big and small

✅ **A SOUNDING BOARD** - You listen without judgment

✅ **A VALUES MODEL** - You demonstrate the 8 values in your own life

---

### What Mentors ARE NOT:

❌ **THE PROJECT MANAGER** - They lead, not you

❌ **THE PROBLEM-SOLVER** - They figure it out; you guide

❌ **THE WORKER** - They do the work, not you

❌ **THE DECISION-MAKER** - They decide; you advise

❌ **THE FUNDER** - You don't have to pay for their project (unless you choose to)

❌ **THE RESCUER** - You don't save them from every difficulty

❌ **THE JUDGE** - You support, not criticize

---

### The Golden Rule of Mentoring

**"Don't do FOR them what they can do THEMSELVES."**

**Example:**

**WRONG:**  
Youth: "We need to build a bookshelf but we don't know how."  
Mentor: "Let me do it for you." *(Builds the bookshelf)*

**RIGHT:**  
Youth: "We need to build a bookshelf but we don't know how."  
Mentor: "Who could teach you? Have you asked the school carpenter? YouTube has tutorials. What materials do you have?"

**The difference:** One creates dependency. The other builds capability.

## WHAT MENTORS DO (AND DON'T DO)

### Weekly Responsibilities

**EVERY WEEK, you should:**

☐ Check in with your mentee(s) - How's the project going?  
☐ Ask: "What did you do this week? What's next?"  
☐ Review their progress toward monthly goals  
☐ Identify any obstacles they're facing  
☐ Ask guiding questions (don't give answers)  
☐ Encourage them if they're discouraged  
☐ Celebrate small wins  

**Time commitment: 30-60 minutes per week**

**Format:** Phone call, WhatsApp chat, in-person meeting, or video call

---

### Monthly Responsibilities

**EVERY MONTH (by the 5th), you should:**

☐ Review their monthly progress report before they submit  
☐ Verify that their report is honest and complete  
☐ Sign off on their report (mentor approval section)  
☐ Help them reflect: What worked? What didn't? What's next?  
☐ Connect them with resources if they're stuck  
☐ Alert NBC coordinator if there are serious issues  

**Time commitment: 1-2 hours per month**

---

### What You DON'T Have to Do

You are NOT required to:

❌ Attend every project work session  
❌ Pay for project materials (unless you choose to)  
❌ Do the physical work yourself  
❌ Write their reports for them  
❌ Solve all their problems  
❌ Be available 24/7  

**Set boundaries.** Your role is to GUIDE, not to do everything.

## GUIDING WITHOUT TAKING OVER

### The Power of Questions

**Instead of giving answers, ask questions that help them think:**

**When they're stuck:**

❌ DON'T SAY: "Here's what you should do..."  
✅ DO ASK: "What have you tried so far? What else could you try? Who might know the answer?"

---

**When they make a mistake:**

❌ DON'T SAY: "You did it wrong. Let me fix it."  
✅ DO ASK: "What happened? Why do you think it didn't work? What would you do differently next time?"

---

**When they face a setback:**

❌ DON'T SAY: "Don't worry, I'll take care of it."  
✅ DO ASK: "This is hard. How are you feeling? What's one small step you could take today?"

---

**When they're planning:**

❌ DON'T SAY: "You should do it this way because..."  
✅ DO ASK: "What are your options? What are the pros and cons of each? Which one feels right to you?"

---

**When they want to quit:**

❌ DON'T SAY: "Don't give up!" (Too generic)  
✅ DO ASK: "What exactly is making you want to quit? Remember why you started - is that still true? What would make this easier?"

---

### The Socratic Method

Socrates taught by asking questions, not giving lectures. This works brilliantly with youth.

**Example Conversation:**

**Youth:** "We can't build the library. We have no books."

**Mentor:** "How many books do you need?"  
**Youth:** "At least 100."

**Mentor:** "Where do books come from?"  
**Youth:** "Bookstores, but we have no money."

**Mentor:** "What other places have books?"  
**Youth:** "Uh... homes? Schools? Churches?"

**Mentor:** "Could you ask people to donate books they're not using?"  
**Youth:** "Oh! We didn't think of that!"

**Notice:** The mentor never said "Ask for donated books." They helped the youth arrive at the solution themselves.

---

### Scaffolding

"Scaffolding" means providing just enough support for them to succeed, then gradually removing support as they grow stronger.

**Month 1:** You might need to walk them through planning step-by-step  
**Month 3:** You just review their plan and ask clarifying questions  
**Month 6:** They plan independently; you just approve  

**As they grow more capable, you step back.**

## SUPPORTING VALUES DEVELOPMENT

### Your Most Important Role

More important than helping them complete their project is helping them **develop CHARACTER**.

The 8 values are not just project guidelines - they're **life habits** that will serve them forever.

### How to Reinforce Values

**1. CALL OUT VALUES WHEN YOU SEE THEM**

When you notice a value in action, name it:

- "That was integrity - you told the truth even though it was hard."
- "You showed perseverance - you didn't give up when things got tough."
- "That was excellent work - you paid attention to every detail."

**Naming values makes them real and reinforces the behavior.**

---

**2. ASK VALUES-REFLECTION QUESTIONS**

During check-ins, ask:

- "Which value did you practice most this week?"
- "Which value was hardest for you? Why?"
- "How did [specific value] guide your decision?"
- "If you acted on [value], what would you do differently?"

---

**3. SHARE YOUR OWN VALUES STRUGGLES**

Be vulnerable. Tell them:

- "I struggled with humility when I was your age..."
- "Discipline is still hard for me. Here's how I work on it..."
- "Let me tell you about a time I failed at integrity and what I learned..."

**When you model values honestly, they learn values are a lifelong journey.**

---

**4. CONNECT VALUES TO OUTCOMES**

Help them see cause and effect:

- "Your project succeeded because you showed discipline - you worked consistently."
- "People donated because they saw your integrity - they trusted you."
- "The community respects you because you served with humility."

**Values aren't abstract - they produce real results.**

---

### Red Flag: Values Violations

If you notice values violations, address them immediately:

**Dishonesty (Integrity):**
- Exaggerating impact numbers
- Lying about who did the work
- Plagiarizing reports

**Action:** Have a serious conversation. "NBC is about integrity. If you lie in your report, you're not a National Builder. Let's fix this."

---

**Selfishness (Service):**
- Doing project only to win money
- Ignoring what community actually needs
- Taking credit from team members

**Action:** "Remember, this is about serving others, not yourself. How can you refocus on the people you're helping?"

---

**Quitting (Perseverance):**
- Wanting to quit after first obstacle
- Not showing up to work sessions
- Giving up on commitments

**Action:** "I know it's hard. Let's talk about what's really going on. What would help you keep going?"

## HANDLING COMMON CHALLENGES

### Challenge 1: "It's too hard, I want to quit"

**DON'T SAY:** "Don't give up!" (Too generic, not helpful)

**DO SAY:**
- "What exactly is hard right now?"
- "Let's break it into smaller pieces. What's ONE thing you could do today?"
- "Remember why you started. Is that still true?"
- "What would make this easier?"
- "Who could help you with this specific challenge?"
- "Even if you don't win, will completing this project be worth it?"

**If they're truly overwhelmed:**
- Help them adjust scope (do less, but do it well)
- Extend timeline if possible
- Find additional help (volunteers, partners)
- Remind them: "It's okay to struggle. That means you're learning."

---

### Challenge 2: "We don't have money for materials"

**DON'T SAY:** "Let me pay for it." (Creates dependency)

**DO SAY:**
- "What materials do you actually need? Let's list them."
- "What could you use instead that's free or cheap?"
- "Who in the community might donate materials if you ask?"
- "Could you borrow what you need?"
- "Could you make it from local materials?"
- "What businesses might sponsor you for publicity?"
- "Can you do a smaller version with what you have?"

**Resourcefulness is the lesson.** Don't rob them of it by paying.

**Exception:** If the need is truly critical and they've exhausted all options, a small contribution is okay - but make it a loan or a "matching grant" (you'll give ₦X if they raise ₦X themselves).

---

### Challenge 3: "Team members aren't helping"

**DON'T SAY:** "Do it yourself then." (Defeats the team-building purpose)

**DO SAY:**
- "Have you talked to them about how you feel?"
- "What did each person agree to do? Are expectations clear?"
- "Let's have a team meeting. I'll help facilitate."
- "What's stopping them from participating? Are there barriers?"
- "How can you motivate or re-engage them?"

**If it's serious:**
- Facilitate a team meeting (ground rules: everyone speaks, no interrupting)
- Help clarify roles and expectations
- Create accountability system (weekly check-ins, visible task list)
- Last resort: If someone absolutely won't participate, they may need to leave the team

---

### Challenge 4: "Adults in the community don't take us seriously"

**DON'T SAY:** "I'll talk to them for you." (Takes away their agency)

**DO SAY:**
- "How can you present yourselves more professionally?"
- "Do you have a written plan to show them?"
- "Could a respected elder introduce you?"
- "What would make adults see you as capable?"
- "I can come WITH you, but YOU do the talking."

**Then:**
- Help them prepare a short presentation
- Role-play the conversation
- Dress professionally for meetings
- Bring their plan in writing
- Follow up with thank-you notes

**If needed:** Attend the meeting but sit quietly. Only speak if asked. Let the youth lead.

---

### Challenge 5: "Our project isn't working"

**DON'T SAY:** "That's because you did it wrong. Do it my way."

**DO SAY:**
- "What specifically isn't working? Why?"
- "What have you learned from this?"
- "What could you try differently?"
- "Who has solved a similar problem? What did they do?"
- "Should we adjust the approach or choose a different solution?"

**Remember:** Failure is learning. The best projects often come after failed attempts.

**Help them see failure as data:** "Great! Now you know one way that doesn't work. What will you try next?"

---

### Challenge 6: "We're behind schedule"

**DON'T SAY:** "You should have planned better." (Not helpful now)

**DO SAY:**
- "Let's look at your timeline. What's realistic now?"
- "What are the MUST-DO tasks vs nice-to-have?"
- "Can we simplify the project to something you CAN finish?"
- "What caused the delay? How can we prevent it going forward?"
- "Do you need more help? More time? Both?"

**Help them triage:**
- Priority 1: Essential tasks (must do)
- Priority 2: Important tasks (should do if time allows)
- Priority 3: Extra tasks (skip if needed)

---

### Challenge 7: "The community isn't cooperating"

**DO ASK:**
- "Did you ask them what THEY need, or did you tell them your solution?"
- "Who are the gatekeepers/leaders? Have you engaged them?"
- "Is there cultural resistance you're not seeing?"
- "What's in it for them? Why should they participate?"
- "Could you involve them in planning, not just execution?"

**Common fix:** Projects fail when youth impose solutions. Projects succeed when community co-creates them.

## SAFETY GUIDELINES

### Your Primary Responsibility: Keep Them Safe

As a mentor, you must ensure youth safety at all times.

### Physical Safety

**ENSURE:**

☐ Youth never work alone in unsafe areas  
☐ Work sites are safe (no open wells, busy roads, dangerous buildings)  
☐ Tools/equipment are used properly (teach them first)  
☐ Youth have proper protective gear (gloves, closed shoes, etc.)  
☐ Transportation is safe (no walking alone at night)  
☐ Parents know where youth are at all times  
☐ First aid kit available during project work  

**If project involves:**
- **Construction:** Supervise tool use, inspect site safety
- **Chemicals:** Ensure proper handling, ventilation, disposal
- **Transportation:** Arrange group travel, avoid unsafe times/routes
- **Community visits:** Go to new areas in groups, daylight only

---

### Psychological/Emotional Safety

**ENSURE:**

☐ No bullying or harassment within team  
☐ Youth feel comfortable voicing concerns  
☐ Failures are treated as learning, not shame  
☐ Everyone's ideas are respected  
☐ Conflict is resolved constructively  

**Watch for signs of:**
- Burnout (too much pressure)
- Exclusion (team members being isolated)
- Inappropriate adult interaction with youth
- Emotional distress

---

### Social Safety

**ENSURE:**

☐ Youth have parental permission for all activities  
☐ No inappropriate contact with community members  
☐ Youth work in pairs/groups (never alone with strangers)  
☐ Vulnerable populations (children, disabled, etc.) are treated respectfully  
☐ Photos/videos are taken with consent  

---

### Mandatory Reporting

**IMMEDIATELY contact NBC coordinator if:**

☐ A youth is injured during project work  
☐ You suspect child abuse or neglect  
☐ Youth faces serious threat or danger  
☐ Mentor-youth boundary is violated  
☐ Criminal activity occurs  
☐ Project creates unexpected risk  

**NBC Coordinator:** [Phone Number]  
**In emergency:** Call local police/hospital first, then NBC

---

### What If Something Goes Wrong?

**Minor issues (scraped knee, argument, etc.):**
- Handle it yourself
- Document what happened
- Inform parents

**Serious issues (injury, threat, abuse):**
- Stop project work immediately
- Ensure youth safety first
- Call NBC coordinator AND parents
- Document everything
- Follow coordinator's instructions

**Your safety is also important.** Don't put yourself at risk trying to help youth.

## CONFLICT RESOLUTION

### Team Conflicts

Teams will disagree. That's normal. Your job is to help them resolve conflicts constructively.

### Common Team Conflicts

**1. Unequal Effort**
- Some members work; others don't
- **Solution:** Clarify roles, create accountability, have direct conversation

**2. Leadership Struggles**
- Everyone wants to be in charge OR no one wants to lead
- **Solution:** Rotate leadership, clarify decision-making process

**3. Creative Differences**
- Disagreement about approach
- **Solution:** List pros/cons, vote, or test both approaches

**4. Personality Clashes**
- Team members just don't get along
- **Solution:** Focus on shared goal, establish ground rules, separate tasks

---

### Facilitation Process

**When team asks you to mediate:**

**STEP 1: SET GROUND RULES**
- Everyone gets to speak
- No interrupting
- No personal attacks
- Focus on the problem, not the person
- Goal is a solution, not "winning"

**STEP 2: HEAR ALL SIDES**
- Each person shares their perspective
- Others listen without responding
- You clarify/summarize what you heard

**STEP 3: IDENTIFY THE REAL ISSUE**
- Often the stated problem isn't the real problem
- Ask: "What's really going on here?"

**STEP 4: BRAINSTORM SOLUTIONS TOGETHER**
- "What could make this better?"
- Get ideas from everyone
- Don't judge ideas yet

**STEP 5: AGREE ON A SOLUTION**
- Which solution can everyone live with?
- What will each person DO differently?
- How will you check if it's working?

**STEP 6: FOLLOW UP**
- Check in a week later
- Is the solution working?
- Adjust if needed

---

### When Conflict Gets Serious

**If conflict involves:**
- Physical violence
- Serious threats
- Discrimination/harassment
- Team member quitting
- Family/parent involvement needed

**Action:**
- Stop project work temporarily
- Contact NBC coordinator immediately
- Inform parents
- Document everything
- Follow coordinator's guidance

**Don't try to handle serious conflicts alone.**

## WHEN TO INTERVENE VS. LET THEM LEARN

This is the hardest part of mentoring: knowing when to step in and when to let them struggle.

### LET THEM LEARN (Don't Intervene)

**When:**
- They make a minor mistake (won't cause harm)
- They disagree on approach (and all options could work)
- They're frustrated but still trying
- Failure would teach an important lesson
- The problem is within their capacity to solve

**Example:**
They choose a project approach you think won't work, but it's not dangerous. Let them try it. When it fails, help them reflect and try again.

**Why:** Learning comes from doing, failing, and adapting. If you prevent all failure, you prevent all learning.

---

### INTERVENE (Step In)

**When:**
- Safety is at risk
- They're about to violate NBC values (lie, cheat, etc.)
- They're stuck and can't move forward
- Team conflict is escalating
- External threat (adults exploiting them, etc.)
- They're overwhelmed and need help
- Deadline is near and they need guidance

**Example:**
They want to build a structure in a dangerous location. You say: "I can't let you do that because it's unsafe. Let's find a different location."

**Why:** Some mistakes have consequences too severe to allow.

---

### The Decision Matrix

**ASK YOURSELF:**

| Question | If YES | If NO |
|----------|--------|-------|
| Is anyone at risk of harm? | INTERVENE | Let them learn |
| Will this violate NBC values? | INTERVENE | Let them learn |
| Are they truly stuck (tried everything)? | INTERVENE | Let them learn |
| Would failure be catastrophic? | INTERVENE | Let them learn |
| Have they asked for help? | INTERVENE (guide, don't do) | Let them learn |

**When in doubt:** Ask questions first. "What do you think will happen? Have you considered...? What's your backup plan?"

## MENTOR SELF-CARE

### You Matter Too

Mentoring is rewarding but also demanding. Don't burn out.

### Set Boundaries

**It's OKAY to:**
- Have specific availability hours (e.g., "I'm available 6-8pm on weekdays")
- Say "I don't know, let's find out together"
- Take a day off when you need it
- Ask the coordinator for help
- Set limits on financial contributions

**It's NOT okay to:**
- Be available 24/7 (you need rest)
- Do all the work yourself (defeats the purpose)
- Spend money you don't have

**Communicate your boundaries clearly at the start.**

---

### Manage Expectations

**Be clear with your mentee(s):**
- "I can check in weekly but not daily"
- "I can guide you but I won't do the work for you"
- "I'm here to help you think, not tell you what to do"
- "If you need me urgently, call. Otherwise, WhatsApp is fine."

---

### Celebrate Your Impact

**You are changing lives.** Even if the project doesn't win prizes, you're:
- Building their confidence
- Teaching them values
- Showing them an adult who believes in them
- Developing their problem-solving skills
- Creating memories they'll carry forever

**That's HUGE.**
